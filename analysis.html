Body type

How to identify your body balance

Stand in front of a mirror wearing fitted clothing. Note where your shoulders, waist, and hips align. Use a flexible tape measure to confirm proportions. The goal isn’t perfection — it’s understanding the beautiful geometry you already have.

  • Hourglass: Shoulders and hips align with a defined waist.
  • Rectangle: Shoulders, waist, and hips are similar in width.
  • Pear: Hips are wider than shoulders; waist is well-defined.
  • Apple: Midsection is fuller with slender legs.
  • Inverted triangle: Shoulders are broader than hips.

Take a photo and trace the outline to see your shape more clearly. Identifying your balance helps you select silhouettes that celebrate your favorite features.

Colour analysis

Skin tone, undertone, and palette

Work with natural daylight and a clean face. Compare silver and gold jewelry against your skin, then hold up white and cream fabrics. These quick tests reveal whether your undertones lean warm, cool, or neutral.

Warm undertone Cool undertone Neutral undertone

Once you confirm undertone, evaluate the depth of your features — light, medium, or deep. Combine undertone and depth to land on your seasonal palette. That palette guides fabric colors, metals, and even makeup shades that make you glow.

  • Springs: Warm and bright. Think buttercream, melon, aqua, and poppy red.
  • Summers: Cool and soft. Misty blue, lavender, rose quartz, and pewter.
  • Autumns: Warm and muted. Olive, ochre, copper, and deep teal.
  • Winters: Cool and high contrast. Icy pink, cobalt, emerald, and jet black.

Still unsure? Snap a selfie in natural light wearing a white tee. Apply a warm filter, then a cool filter. The one that looks most natural hints at your undertone.

Personal style

Spot your signature aesthetic

Look at your three favorite outfits and list the repeating elements: colors, fabrics, shapes, and moods. Do you reach for sharp tailoring, romantic draping, or bold statements? Those repeating choices point to your core style personality.
